**1. Post-industrial** /ˌpəʊst.ɪnˈdʌs.tri.əl/ (adj): belonging or relating to an economy that is no longer based on heavy industry, such as the making of large machines
- Service industries such as tourism have become more important in the post-industrial age.

**2. OECD** /ˌəʊ.iː.siːˈdiː/ (n): abbreviation for Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development: an international organization whose members are countries with advanced economies and whose aim is to encourage economic growth around the world
- The OECD expects the American economy to slow gradually

**3. Underemployment** /ˌʌn.də.rɪmˈplɔɪ.mənt/ (n): a situation in which someone or something is not used as much as they should be
- Underemployment of skills is one of the biggest concerns among recent graduates

**The fact of not having enough work to do, only working part time, or of having a job that does not use all your skills**

- Unemployment and underemployment are close to 50% in Nicaragua.

**4. Aberration** /ˌæb.əˈreɪ.ʃən/ (n): a temporary change from the typical or usual way of behaving
- **mental aberration** I'm sorry I'm late - I had a mental aberration and forgot we had a meeting today.
- In a moment of aberration, she agreed to go with him

**5. Intractable** /ɪnˈtræk.tə.bəl/ (adj): very difficult or impossible to control, manage, or solve
- We are facing an intractable problem.

**6. Precarious** /prɪˈkeə.ri.əs/ (adj): in a dangerous state because of not being safe or not being held in place firmly
- The lorry was **lodged** (mắc kẹt) in a very precarious way, with its front wheels hanging over the cliff.

**7. Casual** /ˈkæʒ.u.əl/ (adj) [INFORMAL]: Casual clothes are not formal or not suitable for special occasions
- He was dressed in casual clothes - sneakers, blue jeans, and a t-shirt.

**8. Casual** /ˈkæʒ.u.əl/ (adj) [NOT INTERESTED]: not taking or not seeming to take much interest
- The psychologist's attitude seemed far too casual, even **brutal** (tàn bạo).
- Security around the conference hotel seemed almost casual.

**9. Casual** /ˈkæʒ.u.əl/ (adj) [TEMPORARY]: not regular or fixed
- **casual sex** They were having casual sex, but it never became a serious relationship.
- casual labour/labourers/workers

**10. Casual** /ˈkæʒ.u.əl/ (adj) [CHANCE]: not serious, considered, or planned
- **casual observer** To a casual observer, everything might appear normal.
- It was just a casual comment - I didn't mean it to be taken so seriously.

**11. Seasonal** /ˈsiː.zən.əl/ (adj): relating to or happening during a particular period in the year
- Seasonal vegetables
- Seasonal farm work

**9. Fixed-term** /ˌfɪkstˈtɜːm/ (adj): lasting for a set period of time  
- She’s employed on a fixed-term contract.  
- The project was completed within the fixed-term arrangement.  

**10. Theorisation** /ˌθɪə.raɪˈzeɪ.ʃən/ (n): the process of forming theories about something  
- Theorisation of social change has been central to modern sociology.  
- His paper offered a complex theorisation of identity.  

**11. Analytical** /ˌæn.əˈlɪt.ɪ.kəl/ (adj): examining or thinking about the details of something carefully in order to understand it better  
- She has an analytical mind, perfect for solving complex problems.  
- The report takes an analytical approach to the issue.  

**12. Consensus** /kənˈsen.səs/ (n): a generally accepted opinion or decision among a group of people  
- The consensus is that the plan will succeed.  
- There is a consensus among experts that climate change is real.  

**13. Anchor** /ˈæŋ.kər/ (v): to make something or someone stay in one position by fastening them firmly  
- The boat was anchored offshore.  
- He anchored the company’s strategy in strong ethical values.  

**14. Disruptive** /dɪsˈrʌp.tɪv/ (adj): causing trouble or disorder; introducing innovation that changes industries or systems dramatically  
- Disruptive students can affect the whole class.  
- The company is known for its disruptive technologies.  

**15. Abstracted** /æbˈstræk.tɪd/ (adj): showing that you are not paying attention to what is happening around you because you are deep in thought  
- He gave her an abstracted smile while lost in his thoughts.  
- She seemed abstracted during the meeting.  

**16. Communal** /ˈkɒm.jə.nəl/ (adj): shared by a group of people, especially for common use  
- They lived in a communal house with shared facilities.  
- The communal garden is open to all residents.  

**17. Emphasise** /ˈem.fə.saɪz/ (v): to show that something is very important or to give special attention to it  
- She emphasised the importance of teamwork.  
- The report emphasises the need for immediate action.  

**18. Exploration** /ˌek.spləˈreɪ.ʃən/ (n): the act of searching, studying, or investigating something in detail  
- Space exploration has expanded our understanding of the universe.  
- The book is an exploration of cultural differences.  

**19. Alleviation** /əˌliː.viˈeɪ.ʃən/ (n): the process of making something less severe or serious  
- The medicine is for the alleviation of pain.  
- Poverty alleviation programs are essential in developing countries.  

**20. Plausible** /ˈplɔː.zə.bəl/ (adj): seeming likely to be true, or able to be believed  
- His explanation sounded plausible.  
- That’s a plausible reason for her absence.  

**21. Constraint** /kənˈstreɪnt/ (n): something that limits or controls what you can do  
- Time constraints made it difficult to finish the project.  
- Financial constraints stopped them from expanding the business.  

**22. Narrow** /ˈnær.əʊ/ (adj): limited in width, range, or scope  
- They walked down the narrow street.  
- The proposal has a narrow focus.  

**23. Sectoral** /ˈsek.tər.əl/ (adj): relating to a particular sector of the economy or society  
- The government introduced sectoral reforms in education.  
- Sectoral differences exist in wage levels.  

**24. Problematise** /ˈprɒb.lə.mə.taɪz/ (v): to consider or treat something as a problem that needs to be addressed  
- The study problematises traditional views of gender roles.  
- She problematised the assumption that growth is always positive.  

**25. Labour-intensitivity** /ˈleɪ.bər.ɪnˈten.sɪ.və.ti/ (n): the degree to which a process or industry relies on human labour rather than machines  
- Agriculture in many countries is characterised by high labour-intensitivity.  
- The factory reduced its labour-intensitivity by automating production.  

**26. Displace** /dɪˈspleɪs/ (v): to force something or someone out of its usual position  
- Flooding displaced thousands of residents.  
- New technologies may displace traditional jobs.  

**27. Precedented** /ˈpres.ɪ.den.tɪd/ (adj): having occurred before; with an established example  
- The situation was unprecedented in scale, yet still precedented in form.  
- It was a clearly precedented legal ruling.  

**28. Absorb** /əbˈzɔːb/ (v): to take in or soak up energy, information, or a liquid  
- The fabric absorbs moisture well.  
- She quickly absorbed the details of the plan.  

**29. Surplus** /ˈsɜː.pləs/ (n): an amount of something that is more than what is needed  
- The country had a food surplus this year.  
- The budget surplus will be reinvested in public services.

**30. Thesis** /ˈθiː.sɪs/ (n): a long piece of writing on a particular subject, especially for a university degree  
- She wrote her thesis on climate change policy.  
- His thesis was accepted with minor revisions.  

**31. Redundant** /rɪˈdʌn.dənt/ (adj): unnecessary because it is more than needed; no longer employed because a job is not required  
- The factory closed, making 200 workers redundant.  
- Some phrases in the essay are redundant.  

**32. Distribute** /dɪˈstrɪ.bjuːt/ (v): to give something out to several people, or spread over an area  
- The charity distributes food to the homeless.  
- The money was distributed fairly among the winners.  

**33. Pertinent** /ˈpɜː.tɪ.nənt/ (adj): relating directly to the subject being considered  
- She asked several pertinent questions during the meeting.  
- Only include information that is pertinent to the case.  

**34. Determinative** /dɪˈtɜː.mɪ.nə.tɪv/ (adj): having the power or ability to decide or determine something  
- Economic factors were determinative in the election outcome.  
- His determinative role influenced the company’s direction.  

**35. Tension** /ˈten.ʃən/ (n): a feeling of nervousness, pressure, or conflict  
- There was tension between the two countries.  
- The film builds tension before the final scene.

**36. Domain** /dəʊˈmeɪn/ (n): an area of knowledge, activity, or territory  
- Physics is his domain of expertise.  
- The website has its own domain name.  

**37. Broader** /ˈbrɔː.dər/ (adj): wider, more general, or covering more subjects  
- We need to take a broader view of the problem.  
- The broader community supported the initiative.  

**38. Mechanism** /ˈmek.ə.nɪ.zəm/ (n): a system, process, or structure that performs a function  
- The mechanism of the clock is very precise.  
- Negotiation can be a mechanism for conflict resolution.  

**39. Divergence** /daɪˈvɜː.dʒəns/ (n): the process or state of moving away in different directions  
- There is a growing divergence between rich and poor.  
- The divergence of opinions created heated debates.  

**40. Valorisation** /ˌvæl.ə.raɪˈzeɪ.ʃən/ (n): the act of giving value to something or making it more valuable  
- The valorisation of cultural heritage is essential for tourism.  
- His work focuses on the valorisation of local traditions.  

**41. Renegotiate** /ˌriː.nɪˈɡəʊ.ʃi.eɪt/ (v): to discuss and change the terms of an agreement  
- The company renegotiated the contract with suppliers.  
- They want to renegotiate the peace treaty.  

**42. Institutional** /ˌɪn.stɪˈtʃuː.ʃən.əl/ (adj): relating to large organizations such as governments, universities, or banks  
- Institutional support is needed for the program.  
- She works in an institutional setting.  

**44. Geared** /ɡɪəd/ (adj): designed or organized for a particular purpose or type of person  
- The course is geared towards beginners.  
- This program is geared to meet industry needs.  

**48. Voluntary sector** /ˈvɒl.ən.tri ˌsek.tər/ (n): the part of the economy made up of non-profit organizations and charities  
- Many services are provided by the voluntary sector.  
- Funding cuts have affected the voluntary sector.
